## Value Propositions

**For clients:** Agency-quality deliverables at gig-economy prices. Faster turnaround than human freelancers. No technical skill required — describe the outcome, receive the artifact.

**For operators:** Monetize agent teams built for personal productivity. Scale income without scaling hours. The marketplace does the client acquisition.

**For acquirers:** Turnkey agentic services layer. Proven operator network. Validated demand signal. Skip 18 months of R&D.

**Timing thesis:** Thousands of builders are assembling sophisticated agent orchestrations right now with no marketplace to expose them. First mover captures the operator supply side — and the supply side is the defensible asset.

## Customer Segments

**Primary — SMB owners & team leads:** Need decks, docs, marketing materials, dev work. Can't afford agencies. Not technical enough to build or run agents themselves.

**Secondary — Operators / builders:** Technically skilled agent orchestrators (CrewAI, AutoGen, LangGraph, Claude tool-use) looking to monetize capability beyond personal use.

**Tertiary — Acquirer platforms:** Incumbent gig marketplaces (Fiverr, Upwork, Toptal, Freelancer.com) needing agentic capability without building from scratch.

## Exit Strategy Timeline

**Phase 1 — Validate (0–6 mo):** MVP platform. 50 operators, 500 completed gigs. Prove demand exists and agents deliver acceptable quality.

**Phase 2 — Scale (6–18 mo):** CONUS expansion. 500+ operators. Operator specialization tiers. Build the metrics that make corp dev teams pick up the phone: GMV velocity, operator retention, client repeat rate, time-to-delivery vs. human freelancer benchmarks.

**Phase 3 — Position (12–24 mo):** Strategic partnerships with target acquirers. API integrations that make acquisition frictionless. Create FOMO among competing platforms.

**Phase 4 — Exit (18–30 mo):** Acquisition by incumbent gig platform. They get proven agentic capability, trained operator network, validated demand, standardized taxonomy. You get the exit.

## Strategic Analysis: The Acquisition Thesis

### Why the Incumbents Are Stuck

Fiverr and Upwork built their entire infrastructure around human freelancers. Their matching algorithms, review systems, payment flows, and dispute resolution all assume a person on the other end. Retrofitting that for agentic delivery is a massive internal lift, and they know it. Meanwhile their core business is getting squeezed as more people go straight to ChatGPT or Claude for the simple stuff. They need an answer and they need it faster than they can build it.

### What "Prove It" Actually Means

Draft AI shows up with a proven platform, a trained operator network, validated demand metrics, and an API layer that maps cleanly onto their existing architecture. The hardest problem is already solved. The acquisition math becomes straightforward: buy this for X, or spend 18 months and 3X trying to build it internally while Draft AI keeps growing.

The key to making the validation phase work is keeping operator supply quality high enough that the completed-gig metrics tell a compelling story. 500 gigs with a 4.5+ satisfaction rating and a 40% client repeat rate — that's the kind of data that gets a corp dev team on a call. Vanity metrics like registered users won't move the needle. They'll want GMV velocity, take rate, NPS, and time-to-delivery benchmarks against human freelancers.

### Metrics That Trigger Acquisition Interest

- **GMV velocity:** not just total gross merchandise value, but the growth rate
- **Take rate:** sustainable margin that proves the business model works
- **Operator retention:** proves the supply side is sticky, not mercenary
- **Client repeat rate:** proves the output quality justifies coming back
- **Time-to-delivery vs. human freelancer benchmarks:** the core value prop in numbers
- **NPS:** client satisfaction that survives due diligence

### Strategic Fork: Brand-Agnostic vs. Target-Optimized

This decision should be made before writing the first line of platform code.

**Option A — Brand-agnostic (multiple acquirers compete for you):** Build a platform-neutral architecture. Court interest from Fiverr, Upwork, Toptal, and Freelancer.com simultaneously. The competitive dynamic drives up acquisition price. Risk: more complex build, and you may not be a perfect fit for anyone.

**Option B — Target-optimized (build to one acquirer's specs):** Pick the most likely acquirer early. Design your API layer, operator data model, and delivery pipeline to map cleanly onto their existing architecture. Reduces their perceived integration risk and speeds the deal. Risk: if that acquirer passes, you've optimized for one buyer.

Both are defensible. Option A gets a better price through competition. Option B reduces friction and accelerates the timeline.
